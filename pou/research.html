Pou 01 — Rangahau

PhD Research

Understanding what works — for whānau, not just in trials.

The research question

My PhD research focuses on developing a medicine review process for whānau Māori living with type 2 diabetes — one that is grounded in Indigenous values and designed to work in the real contexts of people's lives.

The central question isn't just "what medicines work?" It's: what does a health intervention need to look like — in terms of values, relationships, and design — to actually support sustained hauora for whānau?

Why this matters

Type 2 diabetes disproportionately affects Māori. But the gap in outcomes isn't explained by biology — it's explained by systems. By services that weren't designed with whānau in mind. By clinical encounters that don't account for the realities of people's lives. By a health system that often measures what's easy to measure, not what actually matters.

This research is an attempt to understand what a different approach looks like — and to build evidence for it.

Approach

The research is grounded in kaupapa Māori methodology — which means it's community-led, relationally grounded, and oriented toward Māori self-determination in health. It draws on both Indigenous frameworks and clinical evidence, and is designed to be practically useful — not just academically interesting.
